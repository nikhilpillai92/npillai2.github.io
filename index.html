Featured Exposure-Response Examples

Emax Dose-Response

A classic exposure-response example showing increasing efficacy with higher exposure and a plateau at saturating concentrations.

Article-style summary: In many PK/PD analyses, an Emax model is used to describe how effect rises with exposure and then levels off, helping teams judge whether higher doses are likely to deliver meaningful additional benefit.

Typical interpretation: the drug shows a clear exposure-response relationship, but additional exposure beyond a certain point yields diminishing returns. This is often used to justify dose selection and to discuss the therapeutic window.

  • Useful for efficacy endpoints with a saturating response
  • Helps quantify the maximum achievable effect
  • Supports discussion of dose optimization

Sigmoid Emax for Biomarkers

Illustrates a steeper concentration-effect relationship for a biomarker, often used when response changes sharply across exposure levels.

Article-style summary: A sigmoid Emax model is often favored when the relationship is more sensitive to exposure in the middle of the range, allowing investigators to capture sharper changes in biomarker readouts.

Typical interpretation: the response changes more sharply once exposure reaches a certain threshold. This can be especially relevant for biomarkers that are highly nonlinear in the clinically observed range.

  • Captures steep concentration-effect behavior
  • Useful when small exposure changes trigger large effects
  • Helpful for identifying exposure thresholds

Indirect Response Model

Shows delayed drug effect over time, frequently used to describe changes in biomarkers or disease markers that respond after exposure.

Article-style summary: Indirect response models are useful when drug effect emerges through changes in production or loss of a biological marker, capturing the time lag that is often seen in pharmacodynamics.

Typical interpretation: the effect is not immediate because the drug changes the underlying biological turnover rather than directly altering the response variable. This approach is common for biomarkers and disease progression endpoints.

  • Captures delayed onset and offset of effect
  • Useful for biomarkers driven by turnover
  • Important for longitudinal study interpretation
