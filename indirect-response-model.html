Indirect Response Models: Understanding Delayed Drug Effect Over Time

This article-style example covers indirect response models, which are often used when drug effect occurs through changes in the production or loss of a biomarker.

Some drug effects are not immediate because the treatment changes the underlying biological turnover rather than directly altering the measured response variable. In these cases, indirect response models are often used to describe the time course of effect. These models are central in pharmacometrics when the response appears after a delay or persists after exposure has declined.

Key idea: the effect reflects changes in biological turnover over time.

Why these models are important

Indirect response models are widely used for biomarkers and disease markers that change because the drug alters production or elimination processes. This makes them especially relevant for endpoints where timing matters, such as inflammation markers, lab values, or disease progression features that evolve gradually.

How the framework is interpreted

Unlike direct response models, these frameworks emphasize the dynamic relationship between exposure and the biological system. They help explain why a drug may need time before the effect is seen and why the effect may persist even after concentrations fall. That makes them particularly useful for longitudinal analyses and for understanding the onset and offset of pharmacodynamic effects.

  • Captures delayed onset and offset of effect
  • Useful for markers driven by turnover
  • Important for longitudinal and time-course interpretation

In real-world applications, these models are often part of a broader exposure-response strategy because they tie together pharmacokinetics, biology, and study design in a single narrative.
